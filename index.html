The Issue

Many people are familiar with the idea of stigma. After all, 86% of Americans believe that people with mental illnesses are stigmatized and discriminated against. And there is no denying, great progress has been made in recent years to educate others about mental health. And yet, 41% of adults in the United States still feel uncomfortable asking for help, according to the APA/Harris Poll 2025 Mental Health Awareness Survey. [4][8]

So why are people still so reluctant to open up, even as seeking help has become more socially accepted?

The way we talk about mental illness and how we incorporate the topic of mental health in everyday conversation, whether it's intended to be serious or not, has more of an influence than most may think.

It’s clear: a lack of awareness about the problem isn’t the only issue. The real problem is that people aren't sure of what they can do about it. The reality is, stigmas are more than just what we choose to say. Stigmatizing language can be found in everyday conversations, whether we fully realize it or not. It is also widely spread throughout the media, and can even be seen in law. While everyone can agree that we should avoid using such language, the real problem is that we are surrounded by it on a daily basis. Stigmas have become so normalized that people can use it casually without even noticing, glossing over the harmful message that it's carrying just below the surface. In fact, anyone is prone to making this mistake, even mental health professionals. This can make it difficult to stop it from spreading in language and media, which can quietly influence people’s beliefs about mental illness. It creates a never-ending cycle where language influences beliefs, passing the harmful messaging into media and law, finally coming back full circle to reinforcing previously held beliefs. [7][13][16]

“Often, people avoid or delay seeking treatment due to concerns about being treated differently or fears of losing their jobs and livelihood. That's because stigma, prejudice and discrimination against people with mental illness are still very much a problem.” [1]

Such a variety of widely spread misconceptions can have a myriad of harmful effects, impacting people with a mental illness. Some of these include, but are not limited to, causing unwarranted fear, limiting social and job opportunities, introducing self-stigma and lowering the self esteem among people that have a mental illness, and preventing these people from getting the help that they need. [3][14]

The goal isn’t censorship or surface level awareness; to really reduce stigma, we must all work together to break the cycle. And the first step starts with all of us replacing it with non-stigmatizing language in our day to day conversations. So, let’s set stigmas on fire to make room for better ways of communicating. [5]

Myth 1

People with mental illnesses are violent and unpredictable

Examples in language:

Describing anyone as "psycho", "crazy", “mad”, “insane”, “lunatic”

Why it's harmful:

When these words are used interchangeably to describe both people with a mental illness and those that are behaving extremely and unreasonably, it suggests that the two are the same, even though they aren’t. Spreading this message distances people with mental conditions, especially those with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder, from others due to fear. This often happens when labeling a violent criminal as “crazy”, associating those who actually have a mental illness, even when the vast majority is not violent, with an act that they didn’t commit. In reality, only 3-5% of violent acts are committed by those who have a serious mental condition. Instead, these people are actually ten times more likely to be the victim. [11] [15] [16]

What to do instead:

Use less stigmatizing alternatives when describing something extreme or irrational, such as absurd, bizarre, wild, or foolish. In addition, when referring to someone’s diagnosis, use the correct terminology as opposed to slang. For example, instead of calling someone “schizo”, say “someone with schizophrenia” instead.

Myth 2

Mental illness doesn't have any impact beyond the mind

Examples in language:

Saying ”it’s just in your head”, “it’s not real, it can’t hurt you”, or “snap out of it” to someone with a mental illness

Why it's harmful:

These types of phrases minimize the struggles of those who have a mental illness. They also shift the blame onto the person with the mental illness, suggesting that their symptoms are under their control, and they are the ones that aren’t putting in the effort to get better. In reality, mental illnesses are just like physical ones. Neither are the fault of the patient, and neither can be simply wished away through the power of positivity. The truth is that mental and physical wellbeing are deeply connected, and it’s important to take care of it in every stage of life. For instance, depression can increase the risk of developing chronic illnesses such as heart disease and stroke. On the other hand, these same chronic illnesses can also increase the chances of getting depression. So, even if mental health might not seem like such a big deal, it actually plays an equally important role in maintaining a healthy life as physical health does. [11][19]

What to do instead:

One quick way to double check what you’re about to say before you say it is to replace the mental illness in your sentence with a physical one. After all, you wouldn’t tell someone with diabetes, for example, to just “snap out of it”. The same applies to mental illnesses.

Myth 3

Mental Illnesses are something anyone can relate to and aren’t that serious

Examples in language:

Describing someone without a mental illness as “schitzo”, “psycho”, “OCD”, ”depressed”, or using clinical diagnoses to describe someone without a mental illness.

Why it's harmful:

Throwing around terms used to describe mental illnesses so casually dilutes their meaning into something much less serious. This makes it harder to use the correct terms to have a genuine conversation about mental health, increasing avoidance taking the topic seriously. When people develop a casual attitude towards mental illnesses and the people that have them, it downplays the struggles that they have everyday and blurs the line between real issues which need care and minor inconveniences. This can discourage people from seeking help because it can make them feel as if they are overreacting. After all, “If nobody else thinks it’s a big deal, why should I?” On the flip side, the exact opposite is occurring to people who do not have a mental illness. With the rise of social media, people have more opportunities to connect than ever. However, this overload of information has led to a rise in self-diagnosis. This has become a growing issue because it creates further confusion and makes it even harder to have a genuine conversation about mental health using the correct terminology, since their meanings are being constantly muddled and meddled with. After all, if everyone claims that they have a mental illness, it makes it appear as though they are just a quirk of your individual personality rather than a real issue. Seeing them in this way is dangerous for those who do have a mental illness, because it encourages them to write off any symptoms they have as just part of their individuality, preventing them from seeking help. [17]

What to do instead:

Take language surrounding mental health seriously and respect the weight that these words carry. Also, put in an effort to really understand what a word means before simply using it just because everyone else is. Here is a great resource if you are unsure about a word. Now, I’m not suggesting that you consult a dictionary every time you open your mouth, but you can stick to describing things just as they are without trying to be the comedian if you aren’t certain. For example, instead of saying “bipolar weather”, you could say “indecisive weather” instead.

Myth 4

People with a mental illness are less intelligent and productive members of society

Examples in language:

Describing someone with a mental illness as “mentally unsound or defective”, “loony”, “nuts”, “screw loose”, or “retard”

Why it's harmful:

These are words used to describe someone as not intelligent, suggesting that people with a mental illness are less valuable and that they lack the ability to synthesize complex ideas. When these words are used, they introduce a social hierarchy where people with mental illnesses are placed at the bottom for something that is out of their control. Additionally, it can introduce self-stigmatizing thinking, where people who have mental illnesses begin to believe the stigmas that surround them. By causing them to believe that they are worth less, people with mental illnesses can be led to conclude that they are a burden, that they are undeserving of care, and that they shouldn’t disturb others with their mental health needs. This kind of thinking pushes them into hiding and causes them to feel shame for something that is out of their control. [3][6][14]

What to do instead:

Instead of using these harmful labels and judging people’s intelligence based on their diagnosis, get to know them first before drawing any conclusions, and refer to them by either their preferred name or someone with “___[their diagnosis]___]“ . This might seem intuitive at first. But the truth is, the act of assuming a person’s character and abilities based on immediate surface level observations happens so often that it has its own name: prejudice. Almost all minorities are subject to this, and most people do it unconsciously. While it is harmful, it is deeply rooted in society due to the messaging about these groups that people have been exposed to over time (such as media perpetuating mental health stigmas). These form implicit biases: An untrue or overgeneralized belief about a group of people that manifests itself unconsciously.This makes them especially tricky to catch, since they can happen without you even noticing. If you want to test for any biases you may have, you can do so here. Don’t feel bad if you do though; pretty much everyone has some sort of implicit bias. Luckily, you can begin to silence these biases by noticing them. Take a moment to reflect on what words you would use to describe someone with a mental illness. Are any of them similar to the stigmatizing words we have listed here so far? Here’s a challenge: try counting all of the stigmas that you notice over the next few days, whether they’re internal or external.

Myth 5

A person and their diagnosis are the same thing

Examples in language:

Describing someone with a mental illness as "schizophrenic", “psychotic”, “OCD”, or referring to them solely by their diagnosis.

Why it's harmful:

Identifying people with a mental illness solely by their diagnosis eliminates their individuality, and makes it seem as if there is nothing more to them than their mental condition, flattening down an entire multifaceted person down to a single description. After all, we wouldn’t describe someone who has cancer, for example, as a cancer-ic, so why would we with mental illnesses. It is also overgeneralizing, since each person’s experience is unique. By calling someone by their disorder, it highlights a part of their identity that is out of their control and pushes them away, separating them and creating a feeling of “us” vs “them”. While some don’t mind openly sharing their experiences with having a mental illness, it can be a more difficult subject for others. When calling people by a diagnosis, the topic of mental health is automatically interjected into every conversation about them, which is not something that everyone would want to discuss with everyone else all of the time. How would you feel if something you were uncomfortable discussing was brought up in every conversation about you? [12]

What to do instead:

When referring to someone you know that has a mental illness, it is better to just use their name. Not only will this make everything seem more humanizing, but calling people by their name will make the other person feel good, as well as grabbing their attention, helping you communicate your point more efficiently. However, if you are talking specifically about someone’s mental illness, it is better to say “someone with _[their diagnosis]_”. This way, it separates the mental disorder from the person, making it just one aspect of them rather than their entire identity. [18]
