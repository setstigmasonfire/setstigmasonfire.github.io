The Issue

Many people are familiar with the idea of stigma. In fact, great progress has been made in recent years to educate others and to reduce stigma surrounding mental health. And yet, 41% of adults in the United States still feel uncomfortable asking for help, according to the APA/Harris Poll 2025 Mental Health Awareness Survey. So why are people still so reluctant to open up, even with so much being done to make seeking help more socially accepted?

The reality is, stigmas are more than just what we choose to say. Stigmatizing language can be found in everyday conversations whether we fully realize it or not, widely spread throughout media, and can even be seen in law. While everyone can agree that we should avoid using such language, the real issue is that we are surrounded by it on a daily basis. People have become so accustomed to it that using it feels natural to use it casually, glossing over the harmful message that it's carrying just below the surface. As a matter of fact, people, including mental health professionals, may not even notice when they are being influenced by these stigmas. This can make it difficult to stop it from spreading in language and media, which can quietly influence people’s beliefs about mental illness.

The goal isn’t censorship or surface level awareness; to really reduce stigma, we must all work together to replace it with non-stigmatizing equivalents. So, let’s set stigmas on fire to make room for better ways of communicating.
