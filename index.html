The Issue

Many people are familiar with the idea of stigma. After all, 86% of Americans believe that people with mental illnesses are stigmatized and discriminated against [4]. And there is no denying, great progress has been made in recent years to educate others about mental health. And yet, 41% of adults in the United States still feel uncomfortable asking for help, according to the APA/Harris Poll 2025 Mental Health Awareness Survey [8]. So why are people still so reluctant to open up, even as seeking help has become more socially accepted?

The way we talk about mental illness and how we incorporate the topic of mental health in everyday conversation, whether it's intended to be serious or not, has more of an influence than most may think.

It’s clear: a lack of awareness about the problem isn’t the only issue. The real problem is that we don’t know what to do about it. The reality is, stigmas are more than just what we choose to say. Stigmatizing language can be found in everyday conversations whether we fully realize it or not. It is also widely spread throughout the media, and can even be seen in law. While everyone can agree that we should avoid using such language, the fact is that we is that we are surrounded by it on a daily basis. Stigmas have become so normalized that people can use it casually without even noticing, glossing over the harmful message that it's carrying just below the surface. As a matter of fact, anyone is prone to making this mistake, even mental health professionals. This can make it difficult to stop it from spreading in language and media, which can quietly influence people’s beliefs about mental illness. It creates a never-ending cycle where language influences beliefs, passing the harmful messaging into media and law, finally coming back full circle to reinforcing previously held beliefs.

“Often, people avoid or delay seeking treatment due to concerns about being treated differently or fears of losing their jobs and livelihood. That's because stigma, prejudice and discrimination against people with mental illness are still very much a problem.” [1]

Such a variety of widely spread misconceptions can have a myriad of harmful effects, impacting people with a mental illness. Some of these include, but are not limited to, causing unwarranted fear, limiting social and job opportunities, introducing self-stigma and lowering the self esteem among people that have a mental illness, and preventing these people from getting the help that they need.

The goal isn’t censorship or surface level awareness; to really reduce stigma, we must all work together to break the cycle. And the first starts with all of us replacing it with non-stigmatizing language in our day to day conversations. So, let’s set stigmas on fire to make room for better ways of communicating.
